The Medium is The Message

In this piece McLuhan makes the assertion that when it comes to the social message, the medium is the message. It is more important to understand the effects of the medium itself on human interaction and existence than to understand its contents in order to understand how this medium shapes human society.

This ‘social message’ cannot be separated from the medium itself. By amplifying existing human processes, the medium causes a disruption or enhancement of our interactions, affecting our social and psychological spheres. This disruption is independent of the content of the medium, it happens regardless.

An example he gives is that of electric light and power, which I feel can also be extended to the internet. You see these mediums eliminate the limitations of time and space and facilitate certain kinds of human interaction. I can read a tweet at 3 am written by someone in Brazil 6 years ago. I am getting access to their thoughts without the barrier of time and space, surely that has an affect on my psyche and my relation to humanity that is independent of the contents of that specific tweet. It is not the kinds of stories that print media published that was so important for that historic moment but rather how print media’s existence changed human stories, the way we interact, and the social formations that arose from it. He calls this ‘the action of media’. And states that there is no medium that does not act and create a social message.

“The effects of technology do not occur at the level of opinions or concepts, but alter sense ratios or patterns of perception steadily and without any resistance” (McLuhan, 1964:5) This quote summarises his main argument in expressing that an understanding of how any medium has affected a society must go beyond its contents. And I strongly agree with him for the most part. Where I disagree with is the extent to which he disregards the contents of a medium going so far as to call it ineffectual in creating a social message. I feel that the medium as well as the content create a social message, and the role of the medium has long been downplayed but the remedy to this ignorance is not in downplaying the contents either. The solution is when studying art to not only look at the content but the medium as well, with equal intensity.

He makes the example of how people would ask ‘what’s the painting about?’ and not ‘what’s the house about?’ drawing on this comparison to imply that asking what a painting is about is as silly as asking what a house is about. But this ignores the fact that paintings as a medium are used to convey ideas, concepts or messages, whilst houses are not. He ignores the fact that mediums have different uses and values in society that dictates how we interact with them and in turn how they shape us. A film has stronger powers for communicating ideas of masculinity than a house does. Even in the example he makes of Napoleon stating that three hostile newspapers are more dangerous than a thousand bayonets. But it is not the newspapers that Napoleon is afraid of, it is hostile newspapers, emphasizing that the content is of great importance.
